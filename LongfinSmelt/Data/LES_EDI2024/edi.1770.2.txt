      2025-07-01
      
         Fish entrainment into California's State Water Project (SWP) and Central Valley Project (CVP) has been a source of mortality for native osmerid species, including Delta Smelt (Hypomesus transpacificus) and Longfin Smelt (Spirinchus thaleichthys). While entrained adult and juvenile fishes are collected, identified and enumerated, the facilities design does not allow for a quantifiable estimate of larval fishes being entrained. This knowledge gap makes it difficult for managers to make scientifically informed decisions regarding the endangered Longfin & Delta Smelt. 
         The Larval Entrainment Study (LES) aims to address this gap by providing a high-resolution dataset and insights into the variable abundances of Smelt at risk of entrainment, the community composition surrounding them, and associated water quality data. Our study design utilizes a variety of gear types and maximizes replicates to improve the understanding of larval Smelt abundances within the southern reach of the Sacramento and San Joaquin River Delta, the zone of entrainment for the SWP and CVP. 
         Sampling began in 2022 using 10-minute stepped oblique tows of a Smelt Larval Survey (SLS) sled mounted with a 500 μm net. Tows were conducted in West Canal, outside Clifton Court Forebay (CCF), where the risk of entrainment is highest. Sampling was conducted five days a week in 2022 and three days a week in the following years. . Beginning in mid-January 2023, a 940 μm mesh net with the same dimensions as the SLS net was added, with sampling alternating between the two. This provided an intermediate gear between SLS (500 μm) and 20mm (1600 μm) nets.  
         In 2024 we expanded to sample a transect of sites within the Old and Middle River (OMR) corridor. This provides broader spatial resolution and improves our ability to detect Osmerids within the South Delta. Sampling alternated between a high priority day (with intensive sampling in the lower San Joaquin River, SLS stations 809 & 812) and a longer transect day that ran the length of the OMR corridor( at SLS stations 809, 812, 902, 915 & 918). We also began to sample using a bongo sled, which allows two nets to sample side by side. 
         This data also includes our special studies; a day/night comparison conducted in 2022, a gear comparison in 2023, and a second day/night comparison study in 2024.

               In 2022, samples were collected adjacent to Clifton Court Forebay in West Canal. Fifteen stepped oblique tows with a ten-minute duration were conducted five days a week. These tows made use of the SLS tow schedule. According to the depth present at the sampling site, a specific length of line was deployed and then drawn in at regular intervals. For example, a typical depth of 30' saw the 125' of line being deployed and drawn in at 8:56, 7:49, 6:42, 5:35, 4:28, 3:21, 2:14, and 1:07. Reducing the line length draws the sled upward, ensuring that as much of the water column as possible is sampled.
               
               Sampling was otherwise based on Smelt Larval Survey (SLS) techniques, as excerpted below;
               
               "Environmental conditions data, including tide, surface turbidity, surface and bottom conductivity, surface water temperature, and Secchi depth are collected at each station at the time of sampling. A General Oceanics (GO) flowmeter is mounted across the net's mouth to estimate the water volume filtered. At the end of each tow, net contents are washed into a cod-end jar attached at the end of the net. Large debris and adult fish (greater or equal 50 mm) are removed if positively identified. If salmonids are caught, lengths and presence of adipose fin are immediately recorded, and the fish are released gently and alive. The remaining contents in the jar are preserved in 10% formalin for identification in the lab. The distinctively labeled sample jars are taken to the laboratory at the California Department of Fish and Wildlife's Bay Delta Region, Stockton. The complete contents are sorted, and any larval fish present are identified and counted. All fish are identified to species or lowest possible taxon. The first 50 fish of each species from each tow are randomly selected and measured (FL) to the nearest millimeter and the rest enumerated. All Delta Smelt are measured regardless of catch size. All Longfin Smelt are measured unless there is an excessive amount ( greater than 200) then only 100 are measured and the rest are plus counted. Only non-zero catch of species caught are recorded." (Mora et al. 2023)
               
               Tows were grouped into transects. Groups of five tows are identified as A, B, or C. At the end of each transect, the field lead would assess whether conditions would make it likely for sampling to leave the target area. Sampling would then either reset to original starting location adjacent to the facility gates or continue sampling as normal.
               
               References:
               Interagency Ecological Program (IEP), V.G. Mora, J. Oceguera Zavala, and J.A. Jimenez. 2023. Interagency Ecological Program San Francisco Estuary Smelt Larva Survey 2009 – 2023 ver 8. Environmental Data Initiative. https://doi.org/10.6073/pasta/276607dd985336d9796f0625b323b79c (Accessed 2024-08-23).
            
            In 2022, LES used a standard SLS sled and net, described as "a cone shaped net 3.35 meters in length with a mouth area of 0.37 m2. The net itself is composed of 505 microm NitexR and is mounted on a fixed metal tube frame with skis. [...] The net is connected to the frame by a canvas mouth." (Mora et al. 2023) A YSI-ProDSS was used to collect surface temperature (C), surface & bottom conductivity (microS/cm), and turbidity (FNU). A secchi disk mounted to a 2 meter pole was used to collect secchi depth in centimeters. For consistency, secchi depth was always collected by the same person on each sampling day, in the shade and without sunglasses. Flowmeter values were checked after each tow to ensure proper performance consistent with typical conditions. Units returning unusual values were replaced. References; Interagency Ecological Program (IEP), V.G. Mora, J. Oceguera Zavala, and J.A. Jimenez. 2023. Interagency Ecological Program San Francisco Estuary Smelt Larva Survey 2009 – 2023 ver 8. Environmental Data Initiative. https://doi.org/10.6073/pasta/276607dd985336d9796f0625b323b79c (Accessed 2024-08-23).

               In 2024, samples were initially collected adjacent to Clifton Court Forebay in West Canal. From 1/2/2024 to 1/19/2024, the same procedures used in 2022 were utilized, with the exception that sampling was conducted three days a week, generally on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday.
               
               Beginning on 1/19/2024, sampling shifted to encompass a broader geographical region, including a series of Smelt Larval Survey (SLS) stations; 809, 812, 902, 915, and 918. These stations were selected with the intention of developing a broad view of the area at risk of entrainment, beginning at the upper limits where entrainment might occur. On priority days (generally Monday & Wednesday), stations 809 and 812 were sampled intensively with ten tows each of the 500 micron and 940 micron nets. On the final day of the week (usually Friday) sampling was conducted at all five stations (a 'transect' day), with four tows (two of each net) conducted at each site.
               
               On 2/5 the 940 micron net was lost during sampling - no replacement was available, and so sampling resumed using the 500 micron net only. 
               
               Beginning on 2/12/2024, LES sampling shifted to using a bongo sled design. This was the result of extensive work designing and testing a new frame design. Two five hundred micron nets were mounted to the frame side by side. Priority sampling then shifted to sample two stations (809 and 812) with six tows each and transect stations (all five stations, once a week) with two tows each. 
               
               A special study was conducted to examine differences in abundance between diel periods for Longfin Smelt. This comprised two sets of paired night-day sampling in March and April, with sampling occurring at 809 and 812.

            Fish entrainment into California's State Water Project (SWP) and Central Valley Project (CVP) has been a source of mortality for native osmerid species, including Delta Smelt (Hypomesus transpacificus) and Longfin Smelt (Spirinchus thaleichthys). While entrained adult and juvenile fishes are collected, identified and enumerated, the facilities design does not allow for a quantifiable estimate of larval fishes being entrained. This knowledge gap makes it difficult for managers to make scientifically informed decisions regarding the endangered Longfin & Delta Smelt. 
            The Larval Entrainment Study (LES) aims to address this gap by providing a high-resolution dataset and insights into the variable abundances of Smelt at risk of entrainment, the community composition surrounding them, and associated water quality data. Our study design utilizes a variety of gear types and maximizes replicates to improve the understanding of larval Smelt abundances within the southern reach of the Sacramento and San Joaquin River Delta, the zone of entrainment for the SWP and CVP. 
            Sampling began in 2022 using 10-minute stepped oblique tows of a Smelt Larval Survey (SLS) sled mounted with a 500 μm net. Tows were conducted in West Canal, outside Clifton Court Forebay (CCF), where the risk of entrainment is highest. Sampling was conducted five days a week in 2022 and three days a week in the following years. . Beginning in mid-January 2023, a 940 μm mesh net with the same dimensions as the SLS net was added, with sampling alternating between the two. This provided an intermediate gear between SLS (500 μm) and 20mm (1600 μm) nets.  
            In 2024 we expanded to sample a transect of sites within the Old and Middle River (OMR) corridor. This provides broader spatial resolution and improves our ability to detect Osmerids within the South Delta. Sampling alternated between a high priority day (with intensive sampling in the lower San Joaquin River, SLS stations 809 & 812) and a longer transect day that ran the length of the OMR corridor( at SLS stations 809, 812, 902, 915 & 918). We also began to sample using a bongo sled, which allows two nets to sample side by side. 
            This data also includes our special studies; a day/night comparison conducted in 2022, a gear comparison in 2023, and a second day/night comparison study in 2024.
